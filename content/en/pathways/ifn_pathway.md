Type I interferons — primarily IFN-α (interferon-alpha) and IFN-β — are signaling molecules that normally coordinate antiviral defense. They bind to the IFNAR receptor (consisting of IFNAR1 and IFNAR2), activate JAK1 and TYK2, which in turn phosphorylate STAT1 and STAT2. STAT1/STAT2 together with IRF9 form the ISGF3 complex, which activates over 300 interferon-stimulated genes (ISGs).

The crucial connection for paradoxical psoriasis is the "yin-yang" of TNF-α and IFN-α — an interplay experimentally confirmed in 2018 by Navarini and colleagues:

**Step 1 (Trigger)**: A skin injury or infection → keratinocytes release antimicrobial peptides (AMPs, e.g., LL-37) → AMPs form complexes with DNA or RNA from dying cells → these complexes activate plasmacytoid dendritic cells (pDC) via TLR7 and TLR9.

**Step 2 (IFN-α wave)**: Activated pDC produce large amounts of IFN-α → IFN-α activates conventional dendritic cells (cDC).

**Step 3 (TNF-α relay and brake)**: cDC produce TNF-α and IL-23. TNF-α has a dual function: it promotes inflammation on one hand, but on the other hand ensures that pDC mature and STOP their IFN-α production. This "brake" is crucial.

**Step 4 (Chronic phase, classical psoriasis)**: IL-23 stabilizes Th17 cells → IL-17/IL-22 → keratinocyte proliferation → chronic plaques.

**What happens under anti-TNF therapy (paradoxical psoriasis)**: TNF-α is pharmaceutically blocked → the "brake" for pDC is missing → pDC produce IFN-α UNCONTROLLABLY → IFN-α-driven innate inflammation of the skin → paradoxical psoriasis. Since cDC also cannot fully mature without TNF-α, a T-cell-mediated autoimmune reaction does NOT occur. Therefore, paradoxical psoriasis is a fundamentally different disease mechanism from classical psoriasis.

Affected are 2–5% of all anti-TNF-treated patients. An elevated IFN signature is detectable in these patients' blood. The same mechanism also explains why anti-TNF therapy can trigger a lupus-like syndrome in 0.5–1% of cases — SLE (systemic lupus erythematosus) is also an IFN-α-driven disease.

Therapeutically, **TYK2 inhibition** (deucravacitinib) offers a promising approach, as it blocks both the IL-23 signaling pathway (classical psoriasis) and the IFN-α signaling pathway (paradoxical psoriasis) downstream. **Anifrolumab** (anti-IFNAR, directly blocks the IFN-α receptor) is approved for SLE.
