title: BAFF/APRIL — Survival Signals for B Cells

BAFF (B-cell Activating Factor, also called BLyS) and APRIL (A Proliferation-Inducing Ligand) are members of the TNF superfamily and essential for B-cell survival and maturation. Without BAFF, peripheral B cells die within a few days.

BAFF has three receptors: **BAFF-R** (for BAFF only, main receptor for B-cell survival → non-canonical NF-κB pathway via NIK → IKKα → p100 → p52/RelB), **TACI** (binds BAFF and APRIL, regulates immunoglobulin production and class switching), and **BCMA** (binds BAFF and APRIL, mainly on plasma cells → long-term survival).

In Sjögren's syndrome, BAFF levels are elevated both in the blood and directly in the salivary glands. BAFF sources are diverse: infiltrating T cells and B cells, but also the glandular epithelial cells themselves — the actual "victims" of the autoimmune reaction thus produce the factor that keeps the attacking B cells alive. BAFF promotes the expansion of marginal zone B cells, the formation of ectopic germinal centers (structures in the salivary glands where autoantibodies are "perfected"), and the production of anti-SSA/Ro and anti-SSB/La autoantibodies. Chronic B-cell stimulation by BAFF also contributes to the increased risk of MALT lymphomas in Sjögren's patients.

The **BAFF paradox** after rituximab therapy: When B cells are depleted by rituximab (anti-CD20), BAFF levels in the blood paradoxically increase. The reason: Fewer B cells are present to "consume" BAFF via their BAFF receptors → free BAFF accumulates. The consequence: The higher a patient's baseline BAFF levels, the shorter B-cell depletion lasts, because the remaining B-cell precursors regrow faster due to elevated BAFF. This partially explains the only ~50% response rate of rituximab in Sjögren's syndrome.

Therapeutically: **Belimumab** (anti-BAFF) is approved for SLE and reduces naive and activated B cells (while sparing memory B cells). The combination of belimumab + rituximab addresses the BAFF paradox: rituximab depletes existing B cells, belimumab blocks BAFF and thus prevents accelerated repopulation. In a phase II study, this combination showed enhanced and more sustained B-cell depletion in the salivary glands. **Ianalumab** (anti-BAFF receptor with simultaneous ADCC effect, i.e., it also depletes BAFF-R-bearing cells) showed promising phase II results in Sjögren's syndrome with improved salivary flow rate.
