TNF-α (tumor necrosis factor-alpha) is one of the most powerful pro-inflammatory signaling molecules of the immune system. It is primarily released by activated macrophages (arrow: macrophage → TNF-α), but also by T cells, NK cells, and even fibroblasts.

The signaling pathway works as follows: TNF-α binds to its receptor TNFR1 (present on almost all cells) or TNFR2 (mainly on immune cells). Inside the cell, a signaling cascade is triggered: TNFR1 → TRADD (an adapter protein) → TRAF2 → IKK complex (the "key" for NF-κB) → IKK phosphorylates IκB (the NF-κB inhibitor) → IκB is degraded → NF-κB (p50/p65) is released and moves into the nucleus → there it activates over 200 inflammation-related genes, including IL-1, IL-6, IL-8, COX-2, MMPs, and ICAM-1. In parallel, the MAPK pathway (p38, JNK, ERK) is activated via TRAF2. Under certain conditions, TNF-α can also trigger cell death (apoptosis): TRADD → FADD → Caspase-8.

A crucial, often overlooked aspect is the role of TNF-α as a negative regulator of IFN-α: TNF-α causes plasmacytoid dendritic cells (pDC) to mature and thereby lose their ability to produce IFN-α. Simultaneously, TNF-α suppresses the transcription factors IRF7 and NF-κB in pDC that are needed for IFN-α production. When a patient receives an anti-TNF drug, this brake is removed — pDC produce IFN-α uncontrollably, leading to paradoxical psoriasis.

**TNF inhibitors** are the longest-used biologics: infliximab (chimeric monoclonal antibody), adalimumab (fully human antibody), etanercept (a soluble TNF receptor-Fc fusion protein that captures TNF-α), certolizumab pegol (PEGylated Fab fragment without Fc portion), and golimumab. They are approved for RA, psoriatic arthritis, psoriasis, spondyloarthritis, and inflammatory bowel diseases.

**Risks**: Paradoxical psoriasis (2–5% of patients, most common under infliximab at 62% of reported cases), tuberculosis reactivation (which is why TB screening is mandatory before therapy), lupus-like syndrome (0.5–1%), and formation of anti-drug antibodies that can lead to long-term loss of efficacy.
