---
title: NF-κB — The Central Inflammation Switch
category: Pathway

NF-κB (Nuclear Factor kappa-light-chain-enhancer of activated B cells) is one of the most important transcription factors (proteins that switch genes on or off) of the immune system. It controls over 200 genes related to inflammation, immune response, cell survival, and cell death.

In the resting state, NF-κB is held by its inhibitor IκB (Inhibitor of κB) in the cytoplasm (the cell interior outside the nucleus). Upon activation by TNF-α, IL-1β, or bacterial components (TLR ligands), a cascade is triggered: Signals converge on the IKK complex (IκB kinase complex) → IKK phosphorylates IκBα → marked IκBα is degraded by the proteasome → released NF-κB (consisting of subunits p50 and p65) moves into the nucleus → there it activates transcription of TNF-α, IL-1, IL-6, IL-8, COX-2 (important for pain sensation), iNOS (produces nitric oxide), MMPs (cartilage-degrading enzymes), ICAM-1 (adhesion molecule for leukocytes), and RANKL (activates bone-resorbing cells).

A built-in negative feedback normally prevents permanent activation: NF-κB also activates the transcription of its own inhibitor IκBα → newly produced IκBα retrieves NF-κB from the nucleus → signal extinguishes.

Besides the described "canonical" pathway, there is the "non-canonical" NF-κB pathway: NIK → IKKα → processing of p100 to p52 → p52/RelB dimer → activates genes for B-cell survival and lymphoid organogenesis. This pathway is particularly important for **BAFF signaling**.

Particularly relevant in rheumatoid arthritis is the crosstalk between NF-κB and the IL-17 signaling pathway: IL-17 and TNF-α act synergistically through the signaling mediator IκBζ (an atypical IκB), leading to massively enhanced IL-6 induction in fibroblasts. NF-κB is constitutively (permanently) active in RA synovial tissue — one of the reasons why the inflammation becomes chronic.
