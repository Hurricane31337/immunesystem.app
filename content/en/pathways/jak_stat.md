---
title: The JAK/STAT Signaling Pathway (The Universal Cytokine Highway)

---

The JAK/STAT signaling pathway is the central mechanism through which more than 50 different cytokines (signaling molecules) exert their effects in cells. It is essentially the "highway" through which external signals reach directly into the cell nucleus to switch genes on or off.

The process in 6 steps: 1) A cytokine (e.g., IL-6) binds to its receptor on the cell surface. 2) The receptor changes shape and brings two JAK proteins (permanently attached to the receptor) into close contact. 3) The JAKs phosphorylate each other (transphosphorylation) and thereby become active. 4) The active JAKs phosphorylate specific sites on the receptor where STAT proteins can dock. 5) The STATs are phosphorylated by the JAKs, detach from the receptor, and form pairs (dimers). 6) The STAT dimers travel via importin-α-5 into the cell nucleus and switch on target genes there.

There are four different JAK proteins (**JAK1, JAK2, JAK3, TYK2**) and seven STAT proteins (**STAT1–4, STAT5A, STAT5B, STAT6**). Different cytokine receptors use different JAK combinations and activate different STATs — this explains why each cytokine has its own effect, even though they all use the same basic mechanism.

To prevent signals from running uncontrollably, there are three braking mechanisms: **SOCS proteins** (Suppressor of Cytokine Signaling) directly block JAKs and mark signaling components for proteasomal degradation. **Phosphatases** like SHP-1 and SHP-2 remove the activating phosphate groups from JAKs. **PIAS proteins** (Protein Inhibitors of Activated STATs) inhibit STAT DNA binding.

Clinically relevant: Over 200 mutations and single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) in JAK/STAT genes are associated with diseases — including RA, atopic dermatitis, and hematological malignancies. The most famous is the JAK2-V617F mutation, which leads to uncontrolled blood cell production (myeloproliferative disorders). JAK3 loss-of-function mutations cause severe combined immunodeficiency (SCID), because without JAK3, neither T cells nor NK cells can mature.
