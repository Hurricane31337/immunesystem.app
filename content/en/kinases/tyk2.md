title: TYK2 — The Most Elegant Therapeutic Approach
category: Kinase

---

TYK2 (Tyrosine Kinase 2) belongs to the JAK family and participates in a limited but immunologically highly relevant group of cytokine receptors: IL-12 receptor (with JAK2 → STAT4, Th1 differentiation), IL-23 receptor (with JAK2 → STAT3/STAT4, Th17 stabilization), type I interferon receptor (IFN-α/β, with JAK1 → STAT1/STAT2, antiviral response), and IL-10 receptor (with JAK1 → STAT3, anti-inflammatory).

A TYK2 inhibitor thus selectively inhibits the IL-12/Th1 axis, the IL-23/Th17 axis, and the type I interferon response — three key pathways in autoimmune diseases — WITHOUT the broad effects of JAK1 or JAK2 inhibition (which would also affect EPO, TPO, IL-6, and many γc cytokines).

**Deucravacitinib** is the first approved TYK2 inhibitor. It works in a particularly elegant way: Instead of blocking the active site (the kinase domain), it binds to the so-called JH2 pseudokinase domain — a regulatory region unique to TYK2. This makes it highly selective for TYK2, without relevantly inhibiting JAK1, JAK2, or JAK3.

The safety profile is accordingly the best among all JAK inhibitors: no increased risk of anemia or thrombocytopenia (since EPO/TPO signaling pathways via JAK2 are unaffected), fewer infections, no increased herpes zoster risk (since the IL-15/NK cell axis via JAK3 remains intact).

Particularly exciting is TYK2 inhibition in the context of paradoxical psoriasis: Since paradoxical psoriasis is driven by uncontrolled IFN-α production and IFN-α signals through TYK2, deucravacitinib could theoretically treat both classical psoriasis (IL-23/Th17 pathway) and paradoxical psoriasis (IFN-α pathway). Additionally, TYK2 inhibition is currently being studied in SLE (lupus), another IFN-α-driven disease.
