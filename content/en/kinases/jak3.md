title: JAK3 — The Immune System-Specific Kinase
category: Kinase

---

JAK3 differs fundamentally from the other three JAKs: It is EXCLUSIVELY coupled to the so-called common gamma chain (γc, also called CD132) — a receptor subunit used only by certain immune cell cytokines. This makes JAK3 a particularly immune system-specific kinase.

The γc-utilizing cytokines and their functions: **IL-2** (T-cell growth and Treg development, → STAT5), **IL-4** (Th2 differentiation and IgE switch, → STAT6), **IL-7** (lymphocyte development in thymus and bone marrow, → STAT5), **IL-9** (mast cell growth, → STAT5), **IL-15** (NK cell development and T-cell memory, → STAT5), and **IL-21** (B-cell differentiation and Th17 function, → STAT3).

When JAK3 is completely absent (through mutations), severe combined immunodeficiency (SCID, "bubble boy" syndrome) results: No functional T cells, no NK cells (since IL-7 and IL-15 fail), B cells are present but functionally defective.

**Tofacitinib** was the first approved JAK inhibitor ever (2012) and inhibits JAK1 and JAK3 (with some JAK2 activity). It is approved for RA, psoriatic arthritis, and inflammatory bowel diseases. The ORAL Surveillance study (a large safety study in RA patients over 65) showed an increased risk of major cardiovascular events (MACE) and malignancies compared to TNF inhibitors, leading to an FDA Black Box Warning. Additionally, the risk of herpes zoster (shingles) is increased under JAK3 inhibition, as IL-15-mediated NK cell function is impaired.
