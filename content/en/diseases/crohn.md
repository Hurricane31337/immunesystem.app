Crohn's disease is a chronic inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) that can affect any section of the digestive tract — from mouth to anus — but most commonly the terminal ileum (the last section of the small intestine) and the colon. The inflammation affects all wall layers (transmural), which can lead to complications such as fistulas (abnormal connecting passages), strictures (narrowings), and abscesses. Symptoms include abdominal pain, chronic diarrhea, weight loss, and fatigue.

Immunologically, Crohn's disease is characterized by an excessive Th1 and Th17 response against the gut flora. In genetically predisposed individuals (including NOD2/CARD15 mutations), the barrier function of the intestinal mucosa is impaired, allowing bacteria to penetrate the intestinal wall. Dendritic cells and macrophages respond with excessive TNF-α, IL-12, and IL-23 production. IL-23 stabilizes Th17 cells that produce IL-17 and IL-22. IL-12 drives Th1 cells that release IFN-γ. The signaling pathways run through JAK2/TYK2 → STAT3/STAT4 (for IL-23/IL-12) and JAK1/JAK2 → STAT1 (for IFN-γ). TNF-α activates macrophages and epithelial cells via NF-κB, driving inflammation and tissue destruction.

Important: Although IL-17 plays a role in Crohn's, anti-IL-17 therapy (secukinumab) paradoxically worsened the disease in studies — presumably because IL-17 also has a protective barrier function in the gut.

**Biologics**: Anti-TNF (infliximab, adalimumab, certolizumab) was the first biologic class for Crohn's and is highly effective. Anti-IL-12/23 (ustekinumab, blocks p40) is approved and well tolerated. Anti-IL-23 (p19): Risankizumab and guselkumab are recently approved and show high efficacy because they suppress Th17 expansion without completely eliminating protective IL-17 function in the gut. Anti-integrins: Vedolizumab (anti-α4β7) blocks immune cell migration into the gut. **JAK inhibitors**: Upadacitinib (JAK1) is approved for Crohn's and works because JAK1 participates in the IL-6, IFN-γ, and IL-23 signaling pathways.
