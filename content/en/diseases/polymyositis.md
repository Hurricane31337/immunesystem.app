title: Polymyositis (Autoimmune Muscle Inflammation)

Polymyositis is an idiopathic inflammatory myopathy (muscle inflammation) in which the immune system attacks skeletal muscle. The cardinal symptom is symmetrical, proximally predominant muscle weakness — meaning the muscles near the hips and shoulders are affected first (climbing stairs, lifting arms become difficult). CK (creatine kinase) in the blood is typically markedly elevated as a marker of muscle damage. A related condition is dermatomyositis, which additionally involves skin changes.

Immunologically, CD8-positive cytotoxic T cells dominate, directly invading muscle fibers and destroying them via perforin/granzyme mechanisms. In polymyositis, muscle fibers express increased MHC class I molecules (normally only present on immune cells), making them targets of the cytotoxic T-cell response. IFN-γ (from Th1 cells, via JAK1/JAK2 → STAT1) and TNF-α drive MHC-I upregulation and inflammation. In dermatomyositis, the type I IFN axis (IFN-α, via JAK1/TYK2) and complement activation at capillaries additionally play a role. Myositis-specific autoantibodies (e.g., anti-Jo-1, anti-Mi-2, anti-MDA5) define clinical subtypes.

**Therapy**: Corticosteroids and immunosuppressants (methotrexate, azathioprine, mycophenolate) are standard. IVIg is effective in refractory cases. **Rituximab** (anti-CD20) is used in refractory myositis, especially in autoantibody-positive forms. **JAK inhibitors** (tofacitinib, baricitinib, ruxolitinib) are increasingly used in refractory dermatomyositis, as they inhibit the IFN signature and T-cell activation — particularly in anti-MDA5-positive dermatomyositis with lung involvement.
