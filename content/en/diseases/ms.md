Multiple sclerosis (MS) is a chronic inflammatory disease of the central nervous system (brain and spinal cord) in which the immune system attacks the myelin sheaths (the insulating covers of nerve fibers). This leads to demyelination plaques that disrupt nerve conduction. Symptoms are diverse: visual disturbances, numbness, muscle weakness, coordination problems, fatigue, and cognitive impairment. MS affects about 2.8 million people worldwide, women two to three times more often.

Immunologically, both Th1 cells (IFN-γ, via JAK1/JAK2 → STAT1) and Th17 cells (IL-17, via IL-23/JAK2/TYK2 → STAT3) play central roles: they cross the blood-brain barrier into the CNS and attack oligodendrocytes (the myelin-producing cells). B cells also play a surprisingly important role — they produce autoantibodies against myelin components, function as antigen-presenting cells for T cells, and produce pro-inflammatory cytokines. The finding that B-cell depletion is highly effective in MS has revolutionized understanding of the disease.

**Biologics and immunotherapies**: **Anti-CD20** (ocrelizumab, ofatumumab) are the most effective biologics in relapsing and primary progressive MS — they deplete B cells and are standard therapy. **Natalizumab** (anti-α4 integrin) blocks immune cell migration into the CNS — highly effective but with PML risk (progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy). Alemtuzumab (anti-CD52) broadly depletes lymphocytes. Interferons (IFN-β) were historically the first MS therapy; they modulate the immune response via JAK1/TYK2 → STAT1/STAT2 — interestingly, IFN-β PROMOTES immunoregulation in MS, while IFN-α is harmful in SLE. **S1P modulators** (fingolimod, siponimod, ozanimod) retain lymphocytes in lymph nodes. Cladribine selectively depletes lymphocytes. Bruton's tyrosine kinase inhibitors (BTKi, e.g., tolebrutinib) are currently being tested in phase III studies and could be the first oral therapy to also reach CNS-resident B cells.
