Pemphigus vulgaris (PV) is a rare, potentially life-threatening autoimmune disease of the skin and mucous membranes. IgG-type autoantibodies target **desmoglein 3** (and desmoglein 1 in cutaneous involvement) — adhesion proteins that connect skin cells (keratinocytes) to each other. When these connections are destroyed by autoantibodies, skin cells separate from each other (acantholysis) → flaccid blisters form that easily rupture, leaving painful erosions. Without treatment, PV can be fatal.

Immunologically, PV is primarily a B-cell and antibody-mediated disease: Autoreactive B cells produce anti-desmoglein IgG antibodies. T helper cells (especially Th2 cells, via IL-4/IL-13) support B-cell differentiation and isotype switching to pathogenic IgG subclasses (IgG4). Antibodies cause acantholysis through both direct disruption of desmoglein binding and intracellular signaling pathways (p38 MAPK, Src kinase) and possibly complement activation.

**Therapy**: **Rituximab** (anti-CD20) has revolutionized PV therapy and is increasingly used as first-line therapy (with short-term corticosteroid pulse) — the RITUX-3 trial showed that rituximab as first-line effectively spares corticosteroids. IVIg is used in severe flares. Mycophenolate and azathioprine as steroid-sparing immunosuppressants. In development: CAR-T cells against BCMA or CD19 (could eliminate long-lived autoreactive plasma cells), anti-FcRn (efgartigimod, accelerates IgG degradation), and anti-CD19 antibodies.
