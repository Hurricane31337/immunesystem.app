Sjögren's syndrome is a chronic autoimmune disease in which the immune system attacks its own glands — primarily the salivary and lacrimal glands. The result is often agonizing dry mouth (xerostomia) and dry eyes (xerophthalmia), collectively known as "sicca symptoms" (from Latin "siccus" = dry). The disease affects about 0.1–0.5% of the population, nine times more often women than men, typically at age 40–60. One distinguishes primary Sjögren's syndrome (independent disease) from secondary Sjögren's syndrome (accompanying another autoimmune disease such as RA or SLE).

Beyond dryness, systemic symptoms can occur: chronic fatigue, joint pain, skin changes, neuropathy (nerve damage), interstitial lung disease, and kidney involvement.

How does the disease develop? In healthy individuals, glandular epithelial cells (cells lining the gland ducts) are inconspicuous and produce saliva or tears. In Sjögren's syndrome, these epithelial cells — presumably through viral infection, genetic predisposition, and hormonal factors — are set into a permanently activated state. The activated epithelial cells begin producing signaling molecules that don't normally belong to them: **BAFF** (a survival factor for B cells), **CXCL13** (a chemokine attracting B cells), and **type I interferons** (IFN-α). Thus they themselves become drivers of the autoimmune reaction.

Plasmacytoid dendritic cells (pDC) migrate into the glands and produce IFN-α there. This IFN-α signal is mediated through the JAK1/TYK2 → STAT1/STAT2 signaling pathway and leads to activation of hundreds of interferon-stimulated genes (ISGs) — the so-called "IFN signature," detectable in Sjögren's patients' blood and glandular tissue.

Through elevated BAFF levels, B cells are attracted to the glands and kept alive there. Particularly marginal zone B cells (MZ B cells, a specialized B-cell subtype) proliferate uncontrollably. In the glands, so-called ectopic germinal centers form — structures that normally occur only in lymph nodes and in which antibodies are "perfected." Here the typical Sjögren's autoantibodies develop: **anti-SSA/Ro** and **anti-SSB/La** (antibodies against the body's own RNA-binding proteins) as well as rheumatoid factor (RF). In parallel, T cells infiltrate the glandular tissue: Th1 cells produce IFN-γ, Th17 cells IL-17 — both contribute to tissue destruction.

The most serious complication is **MALT lymphoma** (a non-Hodgkin lymphoma of mucosa-associated lymphoid tissue): About 5% of Sjögren's patients develop such a lymphoma during their lifetime — the risk is thus 15–20-fold increased. It arises from chronic, BAFF-driven B-cell stimulation.

Why does rituximab (anti-CD20) work in only half the patients? Rituximab destroys CD20-positive B cells, but several factors limit its efficacy: First, long-lived plasma cells do not carry CD20 on their surface and continue producing autoantibodies. Second, after rituximab-induced B-cell depletion, BAFF levels paradoxically increase (because fewer B cells are present to "consume" BAFF), accelerating B-cell repopulation. Third, BAFF expression in the glandular tissue itself (especially from epithelial cells) is so high that it can weaken local rituximab-mediated B-cell destruction.

Why does **upadacitinib** (JAK1 inhibitor) work? Upadacitinib is currently being studied in Sjögren's syndrome and shows promising results because JAK1 participates in multiple disease-relevant signaling pathways: It inhibits the IFN-α pathway (JAK1/TYK2 → STAT1/2, the "IFN signature"), the IL-6 pathway (JAK1/JAK2 → STAT3), the IFN-γ pathway (JAK1/JAK2 → STAT1), and the γc cytokine pathways (JAK1/JAK3, including IL-21). By blocking JAK1, the IFN signature, B-cell activation, and T-cell-mediated tissue destruction are simultaneously dampened.

The most promising strategy is currently the combination of **belimumab** (anti-BAFF) + **rituximab** (anti-CD20): Rituximab depletes existing B cells, and belimumab prevents the BAFF-accelerated repopulation. In a phase II study (Bowman et al., JCI Insight 2022), this combination showed nearly complete B-cell depletion in the salivary glands and more sustained clinical effect than either drug alone. **Ianalumab** (anti-BAFF receptor with ADCC function) also showed promising phase II results with improved salivary flow rate.
