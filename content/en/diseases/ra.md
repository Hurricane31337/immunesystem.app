Rheumatoid arthritis (RA) is a chronic autoimmune disease that primarily affects the joints — typically symmetrically the small joints of the hands and feet. About 0.5–1% of the world population is affected, women two to three times more often than men. Without treatment, RA leads to progressive destruction of joint cartilage and bone with deformities and loss of function.

The pathogenesis (disease development) proceeds in three phases:

**Phase 1 — Initiation**: Genetic predisposition (especially the HLA-DR4 gene) together with environmental factors (smoking is the strongest known risk factor; periodontitis also plays a role) lead to chemical modification of certain body proteins (citrullination). The immune system recognizes these modified proteins as "foreign" and forms autoantibodies against them: ACPA (Anti-Citrullinated Protein Antibodies) and rheumatoid factor (RF). These autoantibodies can be detectable in the blood years before the first joint complaints.

**Phase 2 — Amplification**: Dendritic cells present citrullinated peptides to T cells in the lymph nodes → T-cell activation → T helper cells support B cells in antibody production → more autoantibodies. T cells, B cells, macrophages, and other immune cells infiltrate the synovial membrane (joint lining).

**Phase 3 — Destruction**: The infiltrated macrophages massively produce TNF-α, IL-1β, and IL-6. These cytokines activate fibroblast-like synoviocytes (FLS), which develop into an aggressive, tumor-like tissue called pannus. The pannus grows into joint cartilage and bone. FLS produce matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs) that enzymatically degrade cartilage, and RANKL, which activates osteoclasts (bone-resorbing cells) and causes bone erosions. IL-17 from Th17 cells synergistically enhances the TNF-α effect on FLS via the signaling mediator IκBζ, massively increasing IL-6 production. IL-6 in turn acts through the JAK1/JAK2 → STAT3 pathway, promoting FLS proliferation, RANKL expression, and CRP (C-reactive protein, an inflammation marker) production in the liver.

**Biologic therapy in RA**: TNF-α inhibitors (infliximab, adalimumab, etanercept, etc.) are typically the first biologic choice after methotrexate failure. Tocilizumab and sarilumab (anti-IL-6 receptor) block IL-6 trans-signaling. Rituximab (anti-CD20) is used after TNF failure. Abatacept (CTLA4-Ig, a fusion protein) blocks T-cell co-stimulation. Anakinra (IL-1 receptor antagonist) is less effective than anti-TNF.

**JAK inhibitors** offer oral alternatives to injectable biologics: tofacitinib (JAK1/3), baricitinib (JAK1/2), upadacitinib (JAK1-selective), and peficitinib (approved in Japan). Upadacitinib (JAK1-selective) works in RA because JAK1 participates in the signaling pathways of IL-6 (JAK1/JAK2), IFN-γ (JAK1/JAK2), and several γc cytokines (JAK1/JAK3) — JAK1 blockade thus simultaneously interrupts IL-6-mediated FLS activation, IFN-γ-mediated macrophage activation, and T-cell proliferation.

A notable clinical connection: RA is the most common underlying disease in paradoxical psoriasis under anti-TNF therapy (45% of cases). This means rheumatologists must be particularly vigilant when RA patients develop skin changes under anti-TNF therapy.

**Large joint involvement** (elbows, knees, shoulders, hips): Although RA typically begins in small joints, large joints are also affected during disease progression — most commonly wrist and ankle, followed by knee, elbow, and hip. Studies from the ERAS cohort (Early Rheumatoid Arthritis Study) show that range of motion in all large joints decreases by about 7–13% per year. Interestingly, large joint damage progresses independently of rheumatoid factor status — both RF-positive and RF-negative patients are equally affected. Immunologically, large and small joints show comparable synovial patterns: similar numbers of macrophages, T cells, and fibroblasts, but mechanical stress differences may accelerate cartilage breakdown in large joints.

**RA and Sjögren's syndrome** — frequent overlap: About 5–10% of RA patients (some studies report up to 30%) develop secondary Sjögren's syndrome with dry eyes and dry mouth. Conversely, over 50% of Sjögren's patients have joint pain. The difference: In primary Sjögren's, joint involvement typically causes pain and stiffness without RA's characteristic erosive destruction — the aggressive pannus is absent. Joint involvement in Sjögren's affects similar joints (hands, wrists, knees) but does not lead to deformities. Immunologically, B-cell hyperactivity (BAFF-driven, IFN-α signature) is more dominant in Sjögren's, while TNF-α/IL-6/IL-17-driven FLS activation with pannus formation and bone erosion predominates in RA.
