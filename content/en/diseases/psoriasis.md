Psoriasis is a chronic inflammatory skin disease affecting about 2–3% of the population. Characteristic features are sharply defined, reddened, scaly plaques — most commonly on elbows, knees, scalp, and gluteal cleft. The disease is NOT contagious but autoimmune in nature.

**CLASSICAL PSORIASIS** — the immunological sequence:

The disease typically begins after a trigger (skin injury, infection, stress): Damaged or stressed keratinocytes (skin cells) release antimicrobial peptides (AMPs), particularly LL-37. These AMPs form complexes with DNA and RNA from dying cells. These complexes activate plasmacytoid dendritic cells (pDC), which then produce large amounts of IFN-α (interferon-alpha) — this is the early phase. IFN-α activates conventional dendritic cells (cDC), which then release two crucial signaling molecules: TNF-α and IL-23. TNF-α now has a dual function: it promotes inflammation but simultaneously ensures that pDC mature and stop their IFN-α production — a negative feedback mechanism that temporally limits the IFN-α phase. IL-23 stabilizes and expands Th17 cells, which produce IL-17A, IL-17F, and IL-22. IL-17 acts on keratinocytes: it activates chemokine production (CXCL1, CXCL8 → neutrophil recruitment) and AMPs via ACT1 → NF-κB. IL-22 stimulates keratinocyte proliferation via STAT3 → epidermal thickening (acanthosis). The neutrophils that migrate into the thickened epidermis form the typical Munro microabscesses. Since the proliferating keratinocytes in turn produce AMPs, a positive feedback loop develops that chronifies the disease.

**PARADOXICAL PSORIASIS** — the "counterpart":

Paradoxical psoriasis develops as a side effect of anti-TNF drugs — the very drugs actually used to treat psoriasis. It affects 2–5% of anti-TNF-treated patients. The most common underlying disease is rheumatoid arthritis (45.3% of cases), and the most common trigger is infliximab (62.3%), followed by adalimumab and etanercept.

The mechanism: TNF-α blockade removes the negative feedback described above — pDC can no longer mature → uncontrolled, sustained IFN-α production. Since cDC also cannot fully mature without TNF-α, the usual T-cell-mediated autoimmune reaction does NOT occur. Paradoxical psoriasis is thus an IFN-α-driven inflammation of the innate immune system — WITHOUT T-cell involvement. Histologically, paradoxical lesions show more mast cells, more eosinophils, and more pDC, but FEWER T cells than classical psoriasis lesions. Clinically, it most commonly manifests as palmoplantar pustulosis (pustules on palms/soles), followed by plaque and guttate forms. Genetic risk factors include polymorphisms in the IL-23 receptor gene and a TNF-α promoter SNP (rs1799964).

**Treatment of paradoxical psoriasis**: In mild cases, one can try to "treat through" with topical steroids and vitamin D analogs while continuing anti-TNF. In severe cases, anti-TNF must be discontinued and switched to a different mechanism of action: ustekinumab (anti-IL-12/23), anti-IL-23 antibodies, anti-IL-17 antibodies, or JAK inhibitors are options. Switching to a different anti-TNF drug is NOT recommended, as cumulative worsening is frequently observed.
