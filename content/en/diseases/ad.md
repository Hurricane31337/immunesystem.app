Atopic dermatitis (AD), commonly known as eczema, is the most common chronic inflammatory skin disease. It affects about 15–20% of all children and 2–3% of adults worldwide. Typical features are intensely itchy, reddened, scaly skin areas — in children often on the face and flexural surfaces of arms and legs, in adults frequently on hands, neck, and face. The disease follows a relapsing course.

Its development rests on three interacting factors: a disrupted skin barrier, a misdirected immune response, and an altered skin microbiome (dysbiosis).

**The disrupted skin barrier**: Many AD patients have a genetic predisposition, particularly mutations in the filaggrin gene (FLG). Filaggrin is a structural protein that holds the outermost skin layer together and makes it waterproof. When filaggrin is missing, the skin loses excessive water (increased transepidermal water loss), becomes dry, and permeable to allergens. About 10–30% of AD patients carry an FLG mutation.

**The immune response**: When the barrier is breached, damaged keratinocytes release "alarmins": TSLP (Thymic Stromal Lymphopoietin), IL-33, and IL-25. These activate Th2 cells and ILC2 (innate lymphoid cells type 2), which produce large amounts of IL-4, IL-13, IL-5, and IL-31. IL-4 and IL-13 signal through the IL-4Rα receptor (JAK1/JAK3 → STAT6) with far-reaching consequences: They suppress filaggrin, loricrin, and involucrin (barrier proteins) → the skin barrier becomes even weaker (vicious cycle). They cause B cells to switch to IgE antibody production → elevated IgE levels → allergic sensitization. IL-13 disrupts the ceramide composition of the skin (ceramides are lipid molecules that serve as "mortar" between skin cells). IL-31 (via JAK1/JAK2 → STAT3) acts on nerve endings and causes the tormenting itch. In the chronic phase, Th22 cells (IL-22) and Th1 cells (IFN-γ) join in.

**Dysbiosis**: The weakened barrier promotes overgrowth with Staphylococcus aureus (colonizes >90% of AD patients), whose toxins further amplify the Th2 response.

Therapeutically, understanding of AD has been revolutionized: **Dupilumab** (anti-IL-4Rα) blocks IL-4 and IL-13 simultaneously and improves inflammation, itch, barrier, and dysbiosis. In studies, it reduced water loss, improved filaggrin expression, and normalized ceramides. **Tralokinumab** and **lebrikizumab** selectively block IL-13. **JAK inhibitors** work because the key cytokines IL-4, IL-13, IL-31, and TSLP all signal through JAK/STAT: upadacitinib and abrocitinib (JAK1, oral), ruxolitinib (JAK1/2, cream), baricitinib (JAK1/2, oral) are approved.
