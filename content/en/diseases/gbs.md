Guillain-Barré syndrome (GBS) is an acute autoimmune disease of the peripheral nervous system. It typically begins with tingling and weakness in the legs, spreading upward within days to weeks and potentially leading to severe paralysis — in the worst case with respiratory failure (requiring ventilation). GBS usually occurs 1–4 weeks after an infection (Campylobacter jejuni, CMV, EBV, Zika, COVID-19) or rarely after vaccinations.

The mechanism is **molecular mimicry**: Antibodies formed against surface structures of the pathogen cross-react with gangliosides (lipid molecules) on the surface of peripheral nerves. In the most common form (AIDP, acute inflammatory demyelinating polyradiculoneuropathy), antibodies target myelin components of Schwann cells → complement activation → macrophage recruitment → demyelination. In the axonal form (AMAN), anti-ganglioside antibodies (anti-GM1, anti-GD1a) target the axonal membrane directly → axonal damage. T cells play a subordinate role; the pathogenesis is primarily antibody- and complement-mediated.

**Therapy**: GBS is not treated with biologics per se, but with intravenous immunoglobulins (IVIg, which neutralize autoantibodies and block Fc receptors) or plasmapheresis (exchange of antibody-containing plasma). Corticosteroids are — unlike in many autoimmune diseases — not effective in GBS. In development: Eculizumab (anti-C5, complement inhibitor) is being studied in severe cases, as complement activation is a central pathomechanism. Anti-FcRn antibodies (efgartigimod) could eliminate pathogenic autoantibodies faster through accelerated IgG degradation.
