Ulcerative colitis (UC) is a chronic inflammatory bowel disease that — unlike Crohn's disease — exclusively affects the colon and spreads from bottom (rectum) upward. The inflammation is limited to the mucosa and causes bloody diarrhea, abdominal cramps, and urgency. Long-term, there is an increased colorectal cancer risk.

Immunologically, UC is dominated by an atypical Th2-like response: The intestinal mucosa produces increased IL-13 and IL-5 (via Th2 cells and NKT cells). IL-13 is directly cytotoxic to intestinal epithelial cells and disrupts barrier function — via JAK1/TYK2 → STAT6. In parallel, the TNF-α/NF-κB pathway is activated (macrophages in the mucosa), and Th17 cells contribute to neutrophil recruitment via IL-17. The integrins α4β7 on T cells mediate their migration into the intestinal mucosa via the adhesion molecule MAdCAM-1.

**Biologics**: Anti-TNF (infliximab, adalimumab, golimumab) are first-line biologics. Anti-integrin: **Vedolizumab** (anti-α4β7) selectively blocks lymphocyte migration into the gut — gut-selective, therefore fewer systemic side effects. Anti-IL-12/23: Ustekinumab is approved. Anti-IL-23 (p19): Mirikizumab is approved for UC and shows high response rates. **JAK inhibitors**: Tofacitinib (JAK1/3), upadacitinib (JAK1), filgotinib (JAK1) are approved. Anti-IL-13 approaches (tralokinumab) were studied in UC but showed insufficient efficacy. **S1P modulators**: Ozanimod and etrasimod (modulate sphingosine-1-phosphate receptors and retain lymphocytes in lymph nodes) are a new drug class in UC.
