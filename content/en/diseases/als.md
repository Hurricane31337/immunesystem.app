title: Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis (ALS)

ALS is a progressive neurodegenerative disease in which motor nerve cells (motor neurons) in the brain and spinal cord die. This leads to progressive muscle weakness, muscle atrophy, and ultimately respiratory failure, typically within 3–5 years of diagnosis. ALS is NOT a classical autoimmune disease, but neuroinflammatory processes play an important role in disease progression.

Immunologically, ALS shows complex neuroinflammation: Microglia (the macrophages of the brain) are activated into a pro-inflammatory M1 state and produce TNF-α, IL-1β, and reactive oxygen species that damage motor neurons. Astrocytes are also activated and lose their neuroprotective function. In the blood, elevated levels of pro-inflammatory cytokines and altered T-cell populations are found: In early disease stages, protective Treg cells predominate; in late stages, pro-inflammatory Th1 and Th17 cells dominate — the loss of Treg function correlates with faster disease progression.

Therapeutically, there is currently NO approved biologic for ALS. Riluzole and edaravone are the only approved drugs with modest effect. The following immunological approaches are being studied in clinical trials: Tofacitinib (JAK inhibitor) to inhibit neuroinflammatory signaling pathways, anti-CD40L to modulate microglial activation, low-dose IL-2 for Treg expansion, and masitinib (tyrosine kinase inhibitor, inhibits CSF1R on microglia) showed slowing of progression in certain patient subgroups in phase III. ALS remains one of the greatest unsolved challenges in neurology.
