title: Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE)

SLE is a systemic autoimmune disease that can affect virtually any organ — skin, joints, kidneys, brain, heart, lungs, and blood. Characteristic is the butterfly rash (reddish rash across nose and cheeks), but the disease is extremely variable. It affects about 1 in 1000 people, women nine times more often than men, typically of childbearing age.

Immunologically, SLE is characterized by a loss of tolerance against the body's own nucleic acids (DNA, RNA). Dying cells are not efficiently cleared → released DNA/RNA forms complexes with autoantibodies → these immune complexes activate plasmacytoid dendritic cells (pDC) via TLR7/TLR9 → massive IFN-α production → the so-called "IFN signature" (via JAK1/TYK2 → STAT1/STAT2 → ISGs). IFN-α activates cDC, B cells, and T cells → B-cell hyperactivity with production of anti-dsDNA, anti-Sm, and anti-phospholipid antibodies → immune complex deposition in organs (especially kidney = lupus nephritis) → complement activation → tissue damage. BAFF levels are elevated and promote survival of autoreactive B cells.

**Biologics**: **Belimumab** (anti-BAFF) was the first approved biologic for SLE and reduces B-cell activity and autoantibodies. **Anifrolumab** (anti-IFNAR1) blocks the type I IFN receptor and dampens the IFN signature — particularly effective in skin manifestations and high IFN signature. **Rituximab** (anti-CD20) is effective in clinical practice for refractory disease despite negative phase III trials. **Voclosporin** (calcineurin inhibitor) is specifically approved for lupus nephritis. In development: CAR-T cells against CD19 (have achieved impressive remissions in individual patients), TYK2 inhibitors (deucravacitinib), telitacicept (blocks BAFF and APRIL simultaneously).
