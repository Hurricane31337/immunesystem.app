Chronic inflammatory demyelinating polyradiculoneuropathy (CIDP) is the chronic counterpart of Guillain-Barré syndrome. While GBS runs an acute course and often improves spontaneously, CIDP develops over more than 8 weeks and follows a chronic-relapsing or progressive course. Symptoms include symmetrical weakness in arms and legs, sensory disturbances, and diminished reflexes.

Immunologically, CIDP resembles GBS but progresses more slowly: Autoantibodies (including against paranodal proteins like neurofascin-155, contactin-1, CASPR-1) and T cells infiltrate peripheral nerves and destroy myelin sheaths. Macrophages play an important role in myelin clearance. Pro-inflammatory cytokines like TNF-α and IFN-γ contribute to damage. In the subgroup with anti-paranodal antibodies (IgG4-mediated), the pathogenesis is more strongly antibody-driven.

**Therapy**: Standard therapies are IVIg, corticosteroids, and plasmapheresis. **Efgartigimod** (anti-FcRn) is approved for CIDP and works by accelerating the degradation of IgG antibodies (including pathogenic autoantibodies) — a novel mechanism. **Rituximab** (anti-CD20) is used in refractory cases, especially in the anti-paranodal subgroup. **Rozanolixizumab** (anti-FcRn) is also approved.
