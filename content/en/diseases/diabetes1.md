Type 1 diabetes mellitus (T1D) is an autoimmune disease in which the immune system destroys the insulin-producing beta cells in the islets of Langerhans of the pancreas. Without insulin, the body cannot take up sugar into cells → blood sugar rises → life-threatening ketoacidosis. T1D usually manifests in childhood and adolescence and requires lifelong insulin therapy.

Immunologically, T1D is a T-cell-mediated organ destruction: Autoreactive CD8-positive T cells recognize beta cell antigens (insulin, GAD65, IA-2, ZnT8) and destroy beta cells via perforin/granzyme. CD4-positive Th1 cells produce IFN-γ and TNF-α, which drive inflammation in the islets (insulitis) via JAK1/JAK2 → STAT1 and NF-κB. Autoantibodies (anti-insulin, anti-GAD65, anti-IA-2, anti-ZnT8) are markers of autoimmunity and can be detectable years before clinical manifestation. Treg cells are functionally impaired. A disturbed Th17/Treg balance has also been described.

**Biologics for disease delay**: **Teplizumab** (anti-CD3) is the first approved biologic for delaying T1D onset in high-risk individuals — it modulates autoreactive T cells and can delay the onset of insulin dependence by an average of 2–3 years. In studies: Abatacept (CTLA4-Ig) slows beta cell loss, rituximab (anti-CD20) showed temporary preservation of beta cell function. **JAK inhibitors**: In a remarkable case report, baricitinib (JAK1/2) led to diabetes remission in a patient with STAT1 gain-of-function mutation and T1D. Anti-IL-21 and anti-TNF are also being studied.
