Cutaneous lupus erythematosus (CLE) encompasses a group of lupus manifestations that primarily affect the skin — with or without systemic involvement. There are three main forms: **Discoid lupus erythematosus (DLE)** causes disc-shaped, scarring lesions, especially at sun-exposed sites, and can lead to permanent hair loss. **Subacute cutaneous lupus erythematosus (SCLE)** shows symmetrical, non-scarring, annular or papulosquamous lesions at sun-exposed areas; it is strongly associated with anti-SSA/Ro antibodies and can be drug-induced. **Acute cutaneous lupus erythematosus** shows the classic butterfly rash and is almost always associated with SLE.

Immunologically, the type I IFN axis plays a key role: UV light damages keratinocytes → release of nucleic acids and alarmins → activation of pDC in the skin → IFN-α production → recruitment of cytotoxic T cells and activation of the innate immune system. The signaling pathway runs through JAK1/TYK2 → STAT1/STAT2. In SCLE, anti-SSA/Ro antibodies are pathogenetically relevant: they bind to apoptotic keratinocytes that translocate SSA/Ro antigen to the surface under UV influence, and activate complement cascade and inflammation. SCLE can also be triggered by drugs that enhance the IFN response — including paradoxically anti-TNF drugs.

Therapeutically: Sun protection is essential. Hydroxychloroquine is first-line therapy. **Anifrolumab** (anti-IFNAR) showed particularly strong improvements in skin manifestations in the TULIP studies (measured by CLASI score). **Belimumab** can help with concurrent SLE. **TYK2 inhibitors** (deucravacitinib) are being studied in CLE and could be effective through IFN-α signaling pathway blockade. **JAK inhibitors** (baricitinib, upadacitinib) show efficacy in case reports and small studies.
