Chronic spontaneous urticaria (CSU) is a skin condition in which itchy wheals (urticarial swellings), angioedema (deeper swellings, often in the face), or both repeatedly occur over at least six weeks — without an identifiable external trigger. It affects about 0.5–1% of the population and significantly impairs quality of life.

The disease is driven by activated mast cells in the skin. Mast cells carry the high-affinity IgE receptor FcεRI on their surface. In CSU, there are two main mechanisms of mast cell activation: In **autoallergic CSU** (Type I), the body forms IgE autoantibodies against self-antigens like thyroid peroxidase (TPO) or IL-24. These IgE autoantibodies occupy FcεRI on mast cells and trigger degranulation upon contact with the autoantigen — histamine, prostaglandins, leukotrienes, and cytokines are released. In **autoimmune CSU** (Type IIb), IgG autoantibodies target FcεRI or IgE itself directly and activate mast cells through cross-linking and complement activation (C5a).

Therapeutically, **omalizumab** (anti-IgE) is the only approved biologic for CSU. It binds free IgE and prevents binding to FcεRI. This reduces FcεRI expression on mast cells and basophils within weeks, and mast cell degranulation is suppressed. About 65–70% of patients respond, but 30% remain refractory. For these patients, new approaches are being developed: **Remibrutinib** (BTK inhibitor, inhibits intracellular FcεRI signaling), **barzolvolimab** (anti-KIT/CD117, directly reduces mast cell numbers), and dupilumab (anti-IL-4Rα) is also being studied. Fenebrutinib (BTK inhibitor) also showed efficacy in phase II.
