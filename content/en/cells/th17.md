title: Th17 Cells (The Inflammation Amplifier)

Th17 cells are a relatively recently discovered subset of T helper cells and are now considered central drivers of many autoimmune diseases. A naive T cell becomes a Th17 cell when it simultaneously encounters IL-6 (an inflammatory signal) and TGF-β (Transforming Growth Factor beta) — this is the initiation. Subsequently, IL-23 (from dendritic cells and macrophages) ensures that the Th17 cell is stabilized and proliferates. The intracellular signaling pathway runs through JAK2 and TYK2 to STAT3, and the key transcription factor is RORγt.

Th17 cells PRODUCE (arrow from Th17 away) primarily **IL-17A**, **IL-17F**, **IL-22**, **IL-21**, and **GM-CSF**. These signaling molecules have powerful effects: IL-17A and IL-17F act on skin cells (keratinocytes) and connective tissue cells (fibroblasts), causing them to send out chemokines for neutrophils (a type of immune cell) — CXCL1 and CXCL8 (also called IL-8). IL-22 stimulates skin cell proliferation (via STAT3), which leads to the typical skin thickening in psoriasis. IL-21 helps B cells with antibody production.

In psoriasis, the IL-23/IL-17 axis is THE central disease driver: IL-23 from dendritic cells keeps Th17 cells active → Th17 cells produce IL-17 → IL-17 acts on keratinocytes → keratinocytes proliferate excessively (acanthosis) and attract neutrophils into the skin → typical psoriasis picture. In rheumatoid arthritis, IL-17 and TNF-α work synergistically together (i.e., they amplify each other via the signaling mediator IκBζ), leading to massively increased IL-6 production and driving bone destruction via RANKL (a signaling molecule that activates bone-resorbing cells). In Crohn's disease, Th17 cells also play a role, but caution is needed: anti-IL-17 drugs can paradoxically worsen Crohn's because IL-17 also has a protective barrier function in the gut.

The most important drugs against the Th17 axis are: **Anti-IL-17A antibodies** (secukinumab, ixekizumab), a dual **anti-IL-17A/F antibody** (bimekizumab, blocking both variants simultaneously), and — particularly elegantly — **anti-IL-23 antibodies** (risankizumab, guselkumab), which suppress Th17 proliferation "upstream" (at the source) without completely eliminating the protective IL-17 functions in the gut.
