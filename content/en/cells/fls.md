title: FLS — Synovial Fibroblasts (Joint Destroyers)
category: Cell

Fibroblast-like synoviocytes (FLS) are connective tissue cells that line the synovial membrane (joint lining). In healthy individuals, they provide joint lubrication and nutrient supply to cartilage. In rheumatoid arthritis, however, they transform into aggressively invasive cells that behave almost like tumor cells.

FLS RECEIVE signals (arrows TO FLS): TNF-α (from macrophages) activates the production of matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs — enzymes that break down cartilage) and additional inflammatory mediators via NF-κB and MAPK signaling pathways. IL-6 acts through the so-called "trans-signaling" pathway: soluble IL-6R in the blood binds IL-6 and then docks onto gp130 on FLS → JAK1/JAK2 → STAT3 → cell proliferation and RANKL production. Particularly dangerous is the combination of IL-17 and TNF-α: these two signaling molecules amplify each other through the signaling mediator IκBζ, leading to massively increased IL-6 production.

The result is the **pannus** — an aggressive, tumor-like tissue that grows into joint cartilage and underlying bone. FLS produce MMPs (MMP-1, MMP-3, MMP-13, MMP-14) that break down cartilage, and RANKL, which activates osteoclasts (bone-resorbing cells) and causes bone erosions.

**TNF-α inhibitors** directly reduce FLS activation. **Tocilizumab** (anti-IL-6 receptor) blocks IL-6 trans-signaling. **JAK inhibitors** like tofacitinib inhibit TNF-induced chemokines in FLS, and baricitinib blocks the IL-6 and IFN-γ signaling pathways. Interestingly, anti-IL-17 drugs are less effective in RA than in psoriasis — an indication that signaling pathway weighting differs between diseases.
