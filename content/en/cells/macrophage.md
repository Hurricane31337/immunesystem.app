Macrophages — literally "big eaters" — are cells of the innate immune system found throughout the body. Their main task is to engulf and digest invading pathogens, damaged cells, and waste products (phagocytosis). But macrophages are far more than just garbage disposal: they are central coordinators of the immune response.

When macrophages encounter a pathogen, they undergo fundamental changes. Resting M0 macrophages become so-called M1 macrophages, switching into "alarm mode." In this state, they produce large amounts of signaling molecules (cytokines), particularly **TNF-α** (tumor necrosis factor-alpha), **IL-1β** (interleukin-1-beta), **IL-6** (interleukin-6), **IL-12**, and **IL-23**. These signaling molecules act as alarm signals: they attract additional immune cells, activate inflammatory processes, and instruct other cells on how to respond.

In the diagram, the arrows point FROM the macrophage TO the cytokines — meaning: macrophages PRODUCE these signaling molecules and release them. TNF-α and IL-6 are particularly important because they play a key role in many autoimmune diseases.

In rheumatoid arthritis (a chronic joint inflammation), activated macrophages accumulate in the synovial membrane (joint lining) and flood the joint with TNF-α and IL-6. These signaling molecules in turn activate the connective tissue cells of the joint (fibroblast-like synoviocytes, or FLS), which then release enzymes that attack cartilage and bone. In Sjögren's syndrome (an autoimmune disease of the glands), macrophages migrate into the salivary glands and produce BAFF (a B-cell survival factor), promoting the pathological proliferation of B cells (antibody-producing cells).

Therapeutically, macrophages are an important target: **TNF-α inhibitors** like infliximab or adalimumab capture the TNF-α released by macrophages before it can cause damage. **JAK inhibitors** (Janus kinase inhibitors) like baricitinib block the signaling pathways within cells that are activated by macrophage-derived signaling molecules.
