Dendritic cells are the "sentinels" of the immune system. Their name comes from their tree-like branches (dendrites), with which they scan their environment for dangers. They belong to the antigen-presenting cells — meaning they take up fragments of pathogens and display them on their surface to T cells, initiating the adaptive (learning-capable) immune response.

There are two important subtypes, each shown as a separate cell in the diagram:

**Conventional dendritic cells (cDC)** are the classic sentinels. They patrol through tissues, take up pathogens, and then migrate to the lymph nodes, where they present the captured fragments to naive T cells. In doing so, they produce two crucial signaling molecules: IL-23, which activates Th17 cells (important in psoriasis), and TNF-α. The arrows in the diagram point FROM cDC TO IL-12 and IL-23 — cDC produce these substances.

**Plasmacytoid dendritic cells (pDC)** have a different specialty: they produce massive amounts of type I interferons, particularly **IFN-α** (interferon-alpha). These interferons are normally responsible for antiviral defense. Arrow direction: pDC PRODUCE IFN-α.

Crucial for understanding paradoxical psoriasis is the following mechanism: In the normal course of psoriasis, pDC are first activated and produce IFN-α (early phase). This IFN-α activates cDC, which then release TNF-α. However, TNF-α has an important "braking mechanism": it causes pDC to mature and thereby lose their ability to produce IFN-α. This temporally limits IFN-α production — a negative feedback mechanism.

When a patient receives a drug that blocks TNF-α (e.g., infliximab for RA), this brake is removed. The pDC can no longer mature and continue to produce IFN-α uncontrollably. The result is **paradoxical psoriasis**: a skin inflammation triggered by the anti-TNF drug itself — even though this drug is actually used to treat psoriasis. This paradoxical psoriasis differs fundamentally from classical psoriasis because it is driven by the innate immune system (IFN-α, pDC) rather than by T cells.
