Osteoclasts are large, multinucleated cells that break down bone — normally an important part of bone remodeling. They develop from monocyte precursor cells (a precursor of macrophages) under the influence of two key factors: M-CSF (macrophage colony-stimulating factor) and RANKL (Receptor Activator of NF-κB Ligand).

RANKL is shown in the diagram as a cytokine PRODUCED by FLS and T cells (arrow from these cells to RANKL). RANKL binds to RANK (its receptor on osteoclast precursors) → TRAF6 is activated → NF-κB, MAPK, and the transcription factor NFATc1 are switched on → the precursor cell differentiates into a mature osteoclast. As a counterweight, **OPG** (osteoprotegerin) exists — a "decoy receptor" that captures RANKL before it can bind to RANK.

In RA, this balance is massively disrupted: RANKL is elevated (stimulated by TNF-α, IL-6, and IL-17), while OPG is reduced → uncontrolled bone resorption → the typical joint erosions seen on X-rays. IL-6 induces RANKL on FLS via the STAT3 signaling pathway. IL-17 also enhances RANKL expression via STAT3. TNF-α can even directly stimulate osteoclasts.

**Denosumab** (anti-RANKL) directly inhibits osteoclastogenesis but is not approved for RA. **TNF inhibitors** and **JAK inhibitors** indirectly reduce RANKL-mediated bone destruction by dampening the upstream inflammatory signals.
