Type 1 T helper cells, or Th1 cells, are a specialized subset of CD4-positive T cells (a type of white blood cell). They develop when a naive (undecided) T cell encounters the signaling molecules IL-12 (from dendritic cells) and IFN-γ (interferon-gamma). Under this influence, the cell activates the transcription factor T-bet — a protein that turns on the genes for the "Th1 program." The signaling pathway runs through JAK1/JAK2 and TYK2 to STAT4.

The main task of Th1 cells is fighting pathogens that hide inside cells (e.g., tuberculosis bacteria). For this purpose, they primarily produce **IFN-γ** (interferon-gamma), **TNF-α**, and **IL-2**. The arrows in the diagram point FROM Th1 TO IFN-γ — Th1 cells are the main producers of IFN-γ. IFN-γ in turn activates macrophages so they can more effectively kill pathogens hiding inside them.

In autoimmune diseases, the Th1 response is often misdirected: In rheumatoid arthritis, many IFN-γ-producing Th1 cells are found in the inflamed synovial membrane, where they overstimulate macrophages. In Sjögren's syndrome (inflammation of the salivary glands), Th1 cells dominate in the affected glandular tissue. In paradoxical psoriasis, elevated Th1 cells are also found alongside the dominant IFN-α response.

Therapeutically, Th1 differentiation can be inhibited by blocking the IL-12 signal: **TYK2 inhibitors** like deucravacitinib block the signal transduction of IL-12 (and IL-23) at the cell and thus prevent new Th1 cells from forming. **JAK1/2 inhibitors** like baricitinib can also interrupt the IFN-γ signaling pathway downstream (below the receptor).
