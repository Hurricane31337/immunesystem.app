---
title: Th2 Cells (T Helper 2 Cells)
category: Cell

---

Th2 cells are the "other side" of the T helper response — they normally fight parasites (e.g., worms) and coordinate antibody production. A naive T cell becomes a Th2 cell when it encounters the signaling molecule IL-4. The intracellular signaling pathway runs through JAK1 and JAK3 to the transcription factor STAT6, which in turn activates GATA-3 — the "master program" of the Th2 cell.

Th2 cells PRODUCE (arrow direction: away from Th2) the signaling molecules **IL-4**, **IL-5**, **IL-13**, and **IL-31**. These have the following effects: IL-4 and IL-13 cause B cells to switch to producing IgE antibodies — exactly the antibodies that trigger allergic reactions. IL-5 attracts eosinophils (another type of immune cell). IL-13 stimulates mucus production and alters skin cells. IL-31 causes itching.

In atopic dermatitis (eczema) — one of the most common chronic skin diseases — Th2 cells are overactive. They produce too much IL-4 and IL-13, which weakens the skin barrier (less filaggrin, an important structural protein), excessively produces IgE antibodies, and causes severe itching. In allergic asthma, IL-13 leads to excessive mucus production in the bronchi. In Sjögren's syndrome, IL-4 also promotes pathological B-cell proliferation.

Therapeutically, **Dupilumab** (a monoclonal antibody against IL-4Rα, the shared receptor subunit of IL-4 and IL-13) is one of the most successful biologics in recent years — it blocks both IL-4 and IL-13 simultaneously and is approved for eczema, asthma, and nasal polyps. **Tralokinumab** selectively blocks IL-13. **JAK1 inhibitors** like upadacitinib and abrocitinib inhibit the JAK1 component of the IL-4/IL-13 signaling pathway below the receptor and are approved as oral tablets for eczema.
