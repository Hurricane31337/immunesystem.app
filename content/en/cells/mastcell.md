title: Mast Cells (Allergy Effector Cells)

Mast cells are immune cells that reside in connective tissue and mucous membranes — particularly numerous in the skin, airways, and gut. Their surface is coated with IgE antibodies (via the high-affinity IgE receptor FcεRI). When an allergen binds to these IgE molecules and cross-links them, the mast cell releases the contents of its storage granules within seconds (degranulation): histamine (causes itching, swelling, vasodilation), TNF-α, IL-4, IL-13, tryptase, prostaglandins, and leukotrienes.

In the diagram, mast cells receive signals via IgE (produced by B cells) and IL-33. The arrows from mast cells show that they produce IL-4 and IL-13 (which in turn promotes Th2 responses and B-cell IgE production — an amplification loop in allergies).

In atopic dermatitis (eczema), mast cells in the affected skin area are activated and release histamine → itching. In paradoxical psoriasis (under anti-TNF therapy), histologically significantly more mast cells and eosinophils (another immune cell type) were found compared to classical psoriasis — further evidence that paradoxical psoriasis follows a different mechanism. In rheumatoid arthritis, mast cells in the synovial tissue contribute to pannus formation (aggressive tissue proliferation) through TNF-α and proteases.

**Dupilumab** (anti-IL-4Rα) indirectly reduces mast cell activation because it lowers IL-4/IL-13-mediated IgE production by B cells → less IgE on mast cells → less degranulation.
