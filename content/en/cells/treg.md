---
title: Regulatory T Cells (Treg — The Brake)
category: Cell
color: "#22aa88"
---

Regulatory T cells, or Treg for short, are the "brakes" of the immune system. Without them, every immune response would escalate uncontrollably and destroy the body itself. Treg cells are identified by the surface markers CD4, CD25, and the key protein FoxP3 — a transcription factor that controls the Treg program.

For Treg cells to develop and survive, they need the signaling molecule IL-2. The signaling pathway runs: IL-2 binds to the IL-2 receptor → JAK1 and JAK3 are activated → STAT5 is phosphorylated → STAT5 moves into the nucleus and activates the FoxP3 gene. Treg cells suppress other immune cells through several mechanisms: they produce the inhibitory signaling molecules **IL-10** and **TGF-β**, they express **CTLA-4** (a surface molecule that blocks the activation of other T cells), and they "consume" IL-2 — the growth factor that other T cells need to survive.

In autoimmune diseases, Treg cells are often functionally impaired or numerically reduced. In rheumatoid arthritis, the balance between pro-inflammatory Th17 cells and inhibitory Treg cells is disturbed — in favor of Th17 cells. Interestingly, anti-TNF therapy can partially restore this balance by generating a new Treg population via TGF-β. In psoriasis, fewer functional Treg cells are present in affected skin areas. In Sjögren's syndrome, weakened Treg function enables uncontrolled B-cell proliferation.

Therapeutically, there are experimental approaches to selectively strengthen Treg cells — for example, through low-dose IL-2 (which preferentially expands Treg cells, as they are particularly sensitive to IL-2). **JAK inhibitors** like tofacitinib (JAK1/3 inhibitor) also influence Treg homeostasis through the IL-2 signaling pathway, which can have both desired and undesired effects.
