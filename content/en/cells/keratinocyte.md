title: Keratinocytes (Skin Cells as Immune Players)
category: Cell

Keratinocytes are the main cells of the epidermis (outer skin layer) — they form the protective skin barrier. Long considered passive structural cells, they are now known to actively participate in immune events: they possess receptors for numerous cytokines and themselves produce signaling molecules, chemokines, and antimicrobial peptides (AMPs, small proteins that kill pathogens).

In the diagram, keratinocytes RECEIVE signals (arrows TO keratinocytes): IL-17A and IL-17F bind to the IL-17RA/RC receptor on the keratinocyte → the adapter ACT1 is activated → NF-κB and MAPK signaling pathways are turned on → the keratinocyte then produces CXCL1 and CXCL8 (neutrophil attractants), CCL20 (attracts dendritic cells and Th17 cells), β-defensins and S100 proteins (antimicrobial substances), and LL-37 (an antimicrobial peptide). IL-22 (also from Th17 cells) activates the STAT3 signaling pathway in keratinocytes and stimulates excessive proliferation — this is the cause of skin thickening (acanthosis) in psoriasis. TNF-α activates the production of adhesion molecules (ICAM-1) via NF-κB, allowing more immune cells to migrate into the skin. IL-13 (in atopic dermatitis/eczema) acts via STAT6 and weakens the skin barrier by reducing filaggrin (a structural protein) production.

A key concept in psoriasis is the **positive feedback loop**: Keratinocytes produce AMPs like LL-37 → these form complexes with DNA/RNA from dying cells → these complexes activate pDC → pDC produce IFN-α → IFN-α activates cDC → cDC produce IL-23 → IL-23 activates Th17 cells → Th17 cells produce IL-17 → IL-17 acts on keratinocytes again → more AMPs → amplification cycle.

**Anti-IL-17 drugs** interrupt this loop directly at the keratinocyte. **Anti-IL-23 drugs** interrupt it further "upstream" at Th17 activation. **TYK2 inhibitors** like deucravacitinib inhibit both the IL-23 and IFN-α signals. Topical **JAK inhibitors** like ruxolitinib cream directly inhibit multiple cytokine signaling pathways in the skin simultaneously.
