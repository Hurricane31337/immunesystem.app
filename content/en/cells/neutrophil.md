Neutrophil granulocytes are the most common white blood cells and serve as the "first responders" during infections. They are attracted to the site of inflammation by chemokines (chemical attractants) like CXCL1 and CXCL8 (also called IL-8). There, they phagocytize (eat) pathogens, release antimicrobial substances, and form so-called NETs (Neutrophil Extracellular Traps) — net-like structures made of DNA and enzymes that trap pathogens.

In the diagram, neutrophils receive signals (arrows TO neutrophils): IL-17 acts on keratinocytes and fibroblasts, which then release CXCL8, attracting neutrophils to the site of inflammation. In psoriasis, neutrophils migrate into the epidermis and form the characteristic Munro microabscesses — small collections of pus in the thickened skin. Since the activated keratinocytes send out even more attractants in response to the infiltrating neutrophils, a self-amplifying loop develops. In rheumatoid arthritis, neutrophils accumulate in the joint fluid and release reactive oxygen species (ROS, aggressive oxygen molecules) and proteases (protein-degrading enzymes) that damage joint cartilage.

Direct anti-neutrophil drugs do not exist, but **anti-IL-17 therapies** (secukinumab, ixekizumab) indirectly reduce neutrophil recruitment by interrupting the CXCL8 chemokine signal.
